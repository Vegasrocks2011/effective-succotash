1 · Distance-Time Graphs
What does a distance-time graph show?
Reading a distance-time graph
x-axisTime (s or minutes)
y-axisDistance from start (m or km)
Gradient= speed. Steeper gradient = greater speed.
Flat sectionZero gradient = stationary (not moving). Distance not changing.
Straight lineConstant speed (uniform motion)
Curved lineChanging speed (accelerating or decelerating)
speed = gradient = Δd ÷ Δt

To find speed from a graph: pick two points on the line, draw a right-angled triangle, then divide the vertical rise (Δd) by the horizontal run (Δt).

A distance-time graph cannot have a negative gradient — distance from start cannot decrease (distance is always ≥ 0). If the object returns to the start the line returns to zero.

2 · Velocity-Time Graphs
What does a velocity-time graph show?
Reading a velocity-time graph
x-axisTime (s)
y-axisVelocity (m/s) — can be negative (opposite direction)
Gradient= acceleration. Steep gradient = large acceleration.
Flat sectionZero gradient = constant velocity (zero acceleration)
Area under graph= distance travelled (displacement)
Negative gradient= deceleration (slowing down)
Negative velocity= moving in opposite direction
acceleration = gradient = Δv ÷ Δt
distance = area under graph
For a rectangle: area = base × height
For a triangle: area = ½ × base × height
For a trapezium: break into rectangle + triangle
Key difference from d-t graph: velocity-time CAN have negative values (moving backwards). Distance-time cannot.

3 · Weight
Weight vs Mass
W = m × g
WWeight (N — newtons). A force.
mMass (kg — kilograms). Amount of matter.
gGravitational field strength (N/kg). Earth: g = 10 N/kg. Moon: g ≈ 1.6 N/kg.

Weight vs Mass — key differences

MassAmount of matter. Constant everywhere. Scalar. Measured in kg.
WeightGravitational pull on mass. Changes with location. Vector (downward). Measured in N.
On the MoonMass unchanged; weight is about 1/6 of weight on Earth because g is smaller.
Measured withWeight — newton meter (spring balance). Mass — balance scale.
⚠ Weight is a force — always measured in Newtons (N), never kilograms. Saying "my weight is 60 kg" is scientifically incorrect; that is your mass.
Centre of gravity
Centre of gravity

The centre of gravity (or centre of mass) is the single point through which the entire weight of an object appears to act. For a uniform object it is at the geometric centre. Weight always acts vertically downward from this point.

To find CoG experimentally: hang the object from different points — the CoG lies where the plumb lines intersect.
Objects are stable when their centre of gravity is above their base of support. A low CoG and wide base = more stable.
4 · Hooke's Law
Hooke's Law

"The extension of a spring is directly proportional to the force applied to it, provided the elastic limit is not exceeded."

F = k × e
FForce applied (N — newtons)
kSpring constant (N/m). Stiffness of the spring.
eExtension (m). Extension = stretched length − natural length.
k = F ÷ e  |  e = F ÷ k

Key terms

Natural lengthLength of spring with no force applied
ExtensionIncrease in length when force applied: e = stretched length − natural length
Spring constant kHigher k = stiffer spring. Units: N/m. Gradient of F-e graph.
Elastic limitMaximum force beyond which the spring does not return to original shape (permanent deformation)
Force-extension graph

Reading the graph

Linear sectionStraight line through origin — F ∝ e. Hooke's Law applies. Gradient = spring constant k.
Elastic limitPoint where the line starts to curve. Beyond this, Hooke's Law no longer applies.
Curved sectionNon-linear — extension increases faster than force. Spring is permanently deformed.
Spring constantk = gradient of straight section = F ÷ e
A stiffer spring has a steeper gradient (larger k) — same force gives less extension.
Elastic and inelastic deformation
Elastic deformationObject returns to original shape when force is removed. Within the elastic limit.
Inelastic deformationObject does not return to original shape. Elastic limit exceeded. Permanent change.
Example — elasticStretching a spring within its limit — returns to original length when released.
Example — inelasticOverloading a spring — spring stays stretched even when weights are removed.
Hooke's Law practical — method
Required practical
  1. Hang spring from a clamp stand and measure its natural length
  2. Add masses one at a time and record total force (F = mg)
  3. Measure total length of spring each time
  4. Calculate extension: e = total length − natural length
  5. Plot F (y-axis) vs e (x-axis)
  6. Find gradient = spring constant k
Use a ruler held vertically next to the spring and read at the bottom of the spring to reduce parallax error.
Do not exceed the elastic limit — if the spring doesn't return to its original length, you have gone too far.
The gradient of the linear section of the F vs e graph gives the spring constant k in N/m.
5 · Newton's First Law of Motion
Newton's First Law

"An object remains at rest, or continues to move in a straight line at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a resultant (unbalanced) force."

Resultant force = 0 (balanced forces)

If stationaryRemains stationary. Forces are balanced.
If movingContinues at the same speed in the same direction. Constant velocity.
ExampleCar at terminal velocity — drive force equals drag. Resultant = 0. Constant speed.
ExampleBook on a table — weight downward balanced by normal reaction upward. Stationary.

Resultant force ≠ 0 (unbalanced forces)

EffectObject accelerates in the direction of the resultant force (Newton's 2nd Law: F = ma)
Speed changesObject speeds up, slows down, or changes direction
ExampleCar accelerating — drive force greater than drag. Resultant force forward.
ExampleFalling skydiver before terminal velocity — weight greater than air resistance.
Inertia
Inertia

The tendency of an object to resist changes in its motion. An object at rest tends to stay at rest; an object in motion tends to stay in motion. Inertia is linked to mass — a more massive object has greater inertia and requires a larger force to change its motion (F = ma). Newton's First Law is sometimes called the Law of Inertia.

Seatbelts work because of inertia — in a crash, the car stops but your body tends to continue moving forward at the original speed.
In space, far from gravitational fields and with no air resistance, an object continues moving at constant velocity indefinitely — no force is needed to keep it moving.
